Have you had an experience similar to St. Ignatius in Manresa?

What were your consolations and desolations, and how were you able to discern them within your own context?

My journey as an IT student has included seasons of uncertainty, pressure to succeed, and moments that felt isolating. In those times I confronted uncomfortable and humbling parts of myself—and learned to stay with the process instead of rushing past it.

Consolations showed up as peace, small disciplines that stuck, and a clearer sense of what actually matters beyond grades or recognition. Desolations often looked like intense self-doubt, fear of failure, and a nagging sense of inadequacy that was hard to silence.

Through prayer and honest reflection, I began to read those inner movements more charitably: struggle became less like proof of failure and more like an invitation to grow. I am learning to stay grounded, more aware, and more open to how I am being called to serve.

The Pursuit of Happyness

This film highlights the quiet strength of Chris Gardner as he endures financial instability and repeated rejection while caring for his son. His resilience challenged me to notice where discouragement slows me down—and where persistence rooted in love can carry a person forward.

From an Ignatian perspective, discernment still happens in hardship: choices can move toward magis—what leads to greater meaning and care for others—not only personal achievement.

Resilience, then, is choosing hope and staying grounded in values during silent, difficult seasons. I want to be more intentional, trusting that faithful persistence forms me into a more responsible person.

Soul

Name: Angel Gabriel G. Litob Jr.

Film analysis

Soul

Soul is a film about Joe Gardner, a jazz musician so consumed by his dream that he nearly missed the life already in front of him. What struck me most was the character of 22: a soul who kept telling herself she had no spark, no purpose, no reason to live on Earth. Her self-pity became her own worst enemy, trapping her in a place of endless excuses and resistance. Watching that made me uncomfortable, because somewhere in her story I recognized something in myself.

The scene that stayed with me was seeing how 22's self-pity slowly shaped her into something lost and bitter. It reminded me that when we dwell too long in what we lack, we stop seeing what we already have. Self-pity does not just make us sad, it paralyzes us and keeps us from becoming who we are meant to be.

What I felt watching it was discomfort, and I think that discomfort was honest. It is easy to cheer for Joe from the outside, but harder to admit that I too have moments where I let doubt and self-pity quietly talk me out of moving forward.

My insight about my own soul is this: my purpose right now is to build something meaningful, wealth that gives me the freedom to do what is right, to help, to serve, and to live with integrity. But Soul reminded me that purpose is not just about the destination. It is about refusing to let self-pity become the voice that stops me before I even begin.

Jannah's Story

Name: Angel Gabriel G. Litob Jr.

Film analysis

Jannah's Story

Jannah's story challenges me because she measures service by who she is becoming, while I measure it by what gets done. I serve people in church ministries, I show up, tasks are completed, but I have not been letting service form me from the inside. The work moves forward, but I stay the same.

She also challenges my idea of patience. I wait for results, but only because results confirm I am doing something right. That is not patience, that is self-assurance. Jannah sat with uncertainty and stayed open anyway, without needing proof that her presence was making a difference.

Perhaps the deepest challenge is this: I am more of a helper than a companion. I make things work for people, but I am not sure I am truly walking with them, learning from them, being changed by them, letting their stories touch mine. There is a difference between making someone's ministry run smoother and genuinely accompanying them as a person, and I am not sure I have been doing the latter.

Jannah returned to Bukidnon not just to give, but because that community had already given to her. I have not yet asked myself, what have the people I serve given me? What have I received from them? That is where I need to grow. Not in doing more, but in being more present — open enough to let service leave a mark on me, not just on the tasks around me.

Passion Plan

Name: Angel Gabriel G. Litob Jr.

A Final Summative Assessment

My Passion Plan

I. Personal Mission Statement

Who I Am Becoming
I am becoming a person of conviction and courage-someone who dares to pursue meaningful goals even when the path invites failure, late nights, and moments of looking foolish in the eyes of others. I understand that growth does not come without discomfort, and I choose to embrace that discomfort as the price of becoming who I am called to be. More than building a career, I am building a character: one that remains honest, humble, and hungry for growth regardless of the circumstances around me.
As an Ateneo de Davao graduate, I carry the responsibility not only of technical competence but of conscience-a person who uses knowledge and skill in service of the greater good, rooted in a faith that does justice.

What Drives Me
I am driven by the belief that the quality of your work is a direct reflection of your character. A lazy output signals a lazy spirit; a dishonest output betrays a compromised integrity. I hold myself to a standard of excellence-not perfectionism, but the kind of craftsmanship that comes from genuinely caring about what you produce.
I am also driven by the people I work alongside. I am most alive in the company of individuals who do not treat their work as merely a job-whether they are coders, painters, accountants, or community workers. When someone is genuinely passionate about their craft, it shows: they glow. That energy is contagious, and I want to be someone who both radiates and responds to it.

The Values I Want to Live By
Three values anchor my life and inform every decision I make:
Kindness - I believe in being kind not for reciprocity, but because it is right. A world with more kindness is a world with more dignity, and I want to add to that.
Respect - I respect the dreams and ambitions of others. I will never diminish someone’s aspirations. Instead, I aim to help people find realistic, grounded ways to achieve what they envision.
Growth - I believe a person is either growing or deteriorating; there is no stagnation. Even to stand still is to fall behind. I commit to being a lifelong learner-technically, morally, and spiritually.

The Impact I Hope to Make
I want to attain a level of financial freedom that allows me to choose to do good without having to weigh it against economic survival. True generosity, I believe, is most powerful when it is not constrained. I do not want to give from scarcity-I want to give abundantly.
Beyond personal wealth, my deeper aspiration is to empower others: to give people the opportunity to catch their own fish rather than merely receiving one from me. I want to help people who help other people-to create ripples, not just a single wave. This is the spirit of the ADDU graduate I hope to embody: a man or woman for others, acting from a place of faith that seeks justice.

II. Prayer for My Future Self

Lord,

As I step into the future You have prepared for me, I come before You not with fear of failure, but with a deeper, more honest fear-the fear of losing myself in success.
Guide me to remain who I am when I have nothing: kind, respectful, and willing to learn. Let the victories I gain in the years ahead never become walls that separate me from my values or from the people around me. Let power, recognition, and wealth pass through my hands like water-useful, life-giving, but never something I clutch too tightly.
Lord, grant me patience. Let me be willing to toil, to plant seeds without always seeing the harvest. Let me be ambitious without arrogance-someone who reaches high but stays grounded, who uses confidence to serve and not to dominate.
I ask for temperance-the wisdom to regulate myself, to know when to speak and when to listen, to know when to lead and when to follow. Let me never use my ego as a weapon against the dignity of another person.
Most of all, I ask that in ten, twenty years-when I look back at the road I have walked-I will find that I never traded my character for my career. Let the person I become be someone You recognize, someone my younger self would be proud of.
Not by my strength alone, Lord, but by Your grace.

Amen.

III. My 10–20 Year Passion Plan

Envisioned Profession and Vocation
My vocation sits at the intersection of technical mastery and entrepreneurial leadership. I envision myself growing into a Software Architect or Site Reliability Engineer-roles that carry international credibility and allow me to work at the frontier of technology. In parallel, I intend to grow as a businessman and investor, because I understand that working hard is necessary but not sufficient for building lasting wealth. True financial freedom requires building systems-businesses, investments, and streams of value-that work even when I am not working.
Ultimately, my profession is a vehicle for my deeper vocation: to build a life of purpose and to use the wealth and influence I accumulate to uplift others.

Key Goals, Milestones, and Pathways
Years 1–3: Technical Foundation
- Achieve strong technical proficiency in software engineering (backend systems, cloud infrastructure, SRE practices)
- Build a portfolio of real-world projects that demonstrate depth and problem-solving ability
- Begin contributing to open-source projects and professional communities
- Pursue certifications relevant to cloud platforms (AWS, GCP, or Azure)

Years 3–6: Global Exposure
- Apply for and secure a position abroad-leveraging skills and credibility built in the first phase
- Immerse myself in a high-performance international engineering environment
- Live frugally and intentionally: save, invest, and study business and personal finance seriously
- Begin building the mindset and financial base for entrepreneurship

Years 6–10: Building Systems
- Launch a first business venture-ideally in the technology or services sector
- Transition from working primarily as an employee to building teams and leading organizations
- Begin investing in assets: equities, real estate, or equity in early-stage businesses
- Start giving back: mentoring junior developers, funding scholarships, or supporting community initiatives

Years 10–20: Legacy and Freedom
- Achieve financial independence: income from businesses and investments sustains my lifestyle
- Expand impact: create meaningful employment opportunities and mentorship ecosystems
- Support causes aligned with my values-education, dignity of work, community development
- Serve as a bridge-connecting talented young Filipinos with global opportunities, as someone once gave me that chance

The Values I Will Uphold
Through every phase of this plan, I commit to holding three values as non-negotiable:
Integrity - My word is my bond. My work reflects my character. I will never compromise on honesty, in business or in relationships.
Excellence - I will care deeply about the quality of what I produce. Not for praise, but because the work matters.
Solidarity - I will remember where I came from and who helped me along the way. I will not hoard opportunity-I will multiply it.

The Life I Hope to Build
I want to build a life that is not just successful, but significant. Success is measured by what I accumulate; significance is measured by what I contribute. My goal is to have both-the resources to live well and the character to live meaningfully.
I want to be the kind of person who walks into a room not to take up space, but to create it for others. I want my wealth to be a tool for justice and my skills to be a gift freely given. I want to prove, in my own small way, that a person can be deeply ambitious and deeply good at the same time.
This is not just a plan. It is a promise to myself, to the people who will depend on me, and to the God who has given me the gifts I carry. I will not waste them.
